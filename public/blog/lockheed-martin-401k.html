How 401(k) Plans Are Treated in Divorce

A 401(k) is a defined contribution account, so the central issue is usually the division of an account balance and its investment performance rather than a monthly annuity stream. In most cases, the settlement or judgment states the economic deal, and a separate QDRO is used to make that deal enforceable against the plan.

That matters because the order must be workable for the administrator, not just understandable to the parties. It should clearly identify the plan, define the awarded share, state the valuation date, and explain whether gains and losses apply after that date until segregation or distribution.

For a Lockheed Martin 401(k) case, source-level detail can matter a lot. If the account includes pre-tax, Roth, after-tax, and employer money, the order should make clear whether each source is divided proportionally or under a different stated rule so the tax and economic result match the deal.

QDRO Requirements

A practical QDRO for this plan should identify the participant and alternate payee, name the correct plan exactly, and define the assigned benefit with enough detail for Fidelity or the current review team to implement it without guesswork. For a defined contribution plan, that usually means careful drafting around value, timing, and source balances.

  • Use the exact plan name shown in current statements or administrator records.
  • State the alternate payee award as a fixed dollar amount, percentage, or other defined formula with a clear valuation date.
  • Explain whether post-valuation gains and losses are included until segregation or distribution.
  • Address pre-tax, Roth, after-tax, and employer contribution sources if the intent is to divide them.
  • Review participant loan treatment carefully so the order does not rely on the wrong gross or net account figure.
  • Confirm whether employer contributions are fully vested as of the relevant date before assigning any share of that money.

Division Formulas

There is no single formula that fits every Lockheed Martin 401(k) case. The right approach depends on the settlement, the quality of the available account records, and whether the parties want a fixed-date split or a proportional approach that carries market movement forward to segregation.

  • Fixed percentage as of a stated date: often used when the alternate payee is to receive a defined share of the account as of a valuation date, adjusted for later gains and losses.
  • Fixed dollar amount: useful when the settlement names a specific sum, but the order should still state whether investment performance applies before transfer.
  • Source-by-source proportional division: especially useful when the intent is to divide traditional, Roth, after-tax, and employer sources in the same ratio.

For this plan, source-level clarity is more than a drafting nicety. If the parties intend to divide Roth and non-Roth balances differently, or if employer money is subject to vesting limits, the order should say so directly instead of leaving those issues to later interpretation.

  1. Obtain the latest plan statement and confirm the exact plan title from participant records.
  2. Identify all relevant account sources, including pre-tax, Roth, after-tax, employer money, and any outstanding loan balance.
  3. Confirm the settlement terms: valuation date, awarded share, gains and losses, and whether employer money is included only to the extent vested.
  4. Prepare a draft order that tracks the exact plan name and clearly states source treatment.
  5. Submit the draft for administrator pre-review before court entry whenever possible.
  6. After revisions, obtain court signature and send the entered order through the current submission process for qualification.

Common Mistakes

  • Naming the wrong Lockheed Martin retirement plan or using an imprecise shorthand plan title.
  • Failing to say whether gains and losses apply after the valuation date.
  • Ignoring the distinction between traditional, Roth, and after-tax sources.
  • Overlooking participant loans and drafting from the wrong account value.
  • Assigning employer contribution money without checking whether it was vested on the relevant date.
  • Entering an order before administrator review when no public model language has been found.
