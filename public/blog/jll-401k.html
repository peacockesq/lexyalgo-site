401(k) in Divorce

A 401(k) like the JLL plan is not divided the same way as a pension with a future monthly benefit. It is an account-based plan. That means the order needs to identify what piece of the account is being awarded and how that amount changes, if at all, after the selected valuation date.

In practice, divorce drafting for a defined contribution plan usually turns on five things: the exact plan name, whether the award is a percentage or fixed dollar amount, the valuation date, whether post-date gains and losses apply, and how separate account sources are treated. If any of those points are fuzzy, implementation gets messy fast.

For JLL, the available research makes one issue especially worth flagging: if the participant has both Roth and traditional balances, the order should not leave their treatment to guesswork. Clear language reduces tax-character confusion when the account is segregated or distributed.

QDRO Requirements

A JLL 401(k) domestic relations order should be drafted to satisfy both ERISA requirements and the plan's internal review procedures. The core requirements usually include:

  • Correct identification of the participant, alternate payee, and the plan being divided.
  • Clear statement of the benefit awarded, typically as a percentage, formula-based fraction of the account, or stated dollar amount.
  • A precise valuation date.
  • Direction on whether investment gains and losses apply after the valuation date until segregation or distribution.
  • Language addressing loans, outstanding distributions, and any source-specific treatment if the account contains multiple money types.
Practical point: Exact plan naming matters here. Using the formal name reflected in plan materials helps avoid review delays caused by generic references like “JLL 401(k)” or “company retirement account.”

Division Formulas

For a defined contribution plan, the cleanest formula is usually the one the administrator can calculate from a statement and valuation date without legal interpretation. Common approaches include:

  • Fixed percentage: the alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the vested account balance as of a specific date, adjusted for gains and losses after that date.
  • Fixed dollar amount: the alternate payee receives a stated dollar amount from the account, sometimes with or without later investment adjustments.
  • Source-specific allocation: the alternate payee receives a proportional share of each account source, including traditional and Roth balances, based on the selected valuation date.

When the participant has both Roth and traditional money, it is usually better to state whether the award is drawn proportionally from each source or from only specified sources. That keeps the administrator from having to infer tax treatment.

Steps

  1. Confirm the exact formal plan name from the latest statement, SPD, or administrator materials.
  2. Identify the intended marital division method: percentage, dollar amount, or source-specific allocation.
  3. Select the valuation date and decide whether later gains and losses apply.
  4. Review the participant's statement for Roth, traditional, employer, and loan-related components.
  5. Draft the order using implementation-ready language that the administrator can calculate directly.
  6. Submit a draft for pre-approval if the administrator permits it before filing with the court.
  7. After court entry, submit the signed order and any required administrative forms promptly.

Common Mistakes

  • Using a shorthand or inaccurate plan name instead of the formal JLL plan name.
  • Failing to state whether gains and losses apply after the valuation date.
  • Ignoring Roth versus traditional balances and leaving source treatment unstated.
  • Using a formula that sounds equitable in settlement discussions but is hard for the administrator to implement from account records.
  • Not checking whether an outstanding participant loan affects the balance shown on the valuation date.
  • Waiting too long after court entry to submit the order for qualification review.

FAQ

Does the JLL 401(k) plan usually require a QDRO for divorce division?

Yes. In most divorce cases, a 401(k) plan like this one requires a domestic relations order that qualifies under ERISA and the plan's procedures before an alternate payee can receive a share.

Why does the exact plan name matter in a JLL QDRO?

Because the administrator needs to know exactly which plan is being divided. Using the formal name helps prevent avoidable review delays and back-and-forth over whether the order applies to the correct account.

Should the order address both Roth and traditional balances?

Yes. If both money sources are in the account, the order should say whether the alternate payee receives a proportional share of each source or some different allocation. That protects clarity around tax character and implementation.

Why is the valuation date so important for a 401(k) division?

Because account balances move. Contributions, market performance, transfers, and loans can all change the number. A precise valuation date, plus gain-and-loss language, keeps the calculation anchored.
