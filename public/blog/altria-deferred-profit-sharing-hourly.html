Plan Structure

The Deferred Profit-Sharing Plan for Hourly Employees is a large, well-documented defined contribution plan. The plan holds interests in two Master Trusts — Master Trust A and Master Trust B — which are pooled investment vehicles used across multiple Altria employee benefit plans. As of December 31, 2024, the plan's interests in these Master Trusts totaled approximately $768 million at fair value, with an additional approximately $154 million in fully benefit-responsive investment contracts (stable value). Total net assets were approximately $962 million.

Participants contribute through pre-tax payroll deferrals. Altria (through Altria Client Services LLC) makes employer contributions, which may include a match or profit-sharing allocations. The plan also carries participant loan balances — approximately $19.5 million in notes receivable from participants as of December 31, 2024 — which must be addressed in any QDRO.

Note: This is the hourly plan. Altria maintains a separate Deferred Profit-Sharing Plan for Salaried Employees. The two plans are distinct and a QDRO must name the correct one.

Dividing This Plan in Divorce

As a defined contribution plan, the Altria hourly profit-sharing plan divides the same way as a standard 401(k): the marital portion of the account balance is identified, a QDRO awards the alternate payee a percentage or dollar amount, and the plan segregates the alternate payee's share into a separate account or distributes it.

The Marital Portion

The portion of the account that accrued during the marriage is generally considered marital property. If the participant had a premarital account balance, the parties may use historical account records — available from Fidelity — to trace the separate property portion. State law controls what counts as marital versus separate property, and outcomes can vary significantly by jurisdiction.

Outstanding Loans

The plan carries a meaningful participant loan portfolio. If the participant has an outstanding loan, the account balance shown on statements reflects the gross balance before the loan. The QDRO should address whether the alternate payee's share is calculated based on the gross balance or the net-of-loan balance. Leaving this ambiguous can result in a rejection or a revision request from Fidelity.

Coverture fractions and profit-sharing plans: A coverture fraction is an analytical tool used to calculate the marital share of an account. It is not itself a distribution method. The QDRO specifies the award; the plan administrator (via Fidelity) calculates the dollar value. Parties with significant premarital balances to trace may find a marital-share formula more appropriate than a straight percentage award.

Fidelity's review timeline:

  • Orders prepared using the Fidelity QDRO Center: typically reviewed within 10 business days
  • Orders drafted manually or altered from the generated template: up to 60 business days

At a minimum, the QDRO must:

  • Identify the plan by exact name: Deferred Profit-Sharing Plan for Hourly Employees.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient specificity.
  • State the amount, percentage, or formula for the alternate payee's award.
  • Specify the valuation date.
  • Address gains and losses after the valuation date.
  • Address outstanding loan treatment.
  • Address whether pre-tax and any Roth/after-tax sources are divided pro rata or otherwise.
  • Not require any benefit or form of payment not available under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage of vested account balance

The most common method. The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for investment gains and losses until segregation. Example: "50% of the participant's vested account balance as of [date], adjusted for earnings and losses thereafter until segregated."

Fixed dollar award

A specific dollar amount as of the valuation date. Should explicitly state whether the amount is adjusted for investment experience after the valuation date. Given the plan's Master Trust structure and pooled investments, gains and losses can be meaningful.

Loan-adjusted basis

With roughly $19.5 million in plan-wide participant loans outstanding, loan treatment is not hypothetical. The QDRO should specify whether the alternate payee's share is based on the gross account balance (before deducting the loan) or the net balance. Fidelity's QDRO Center will prompt for this.

Source-specific allocation

If the account contains both pre-tax deferrals and any Roth or after-tax sources, the order should state whether each source is divided at the same percentage or allocated differently. Pro rata across all sources is the common default.

Steps to Divide the Altria Hourly Profit-Sharing Plan

  1. Access Fidelity's QDRO Center at qdro.fidelity.com and locate the Deferred Profit-Sharing Plan for Hourly Employees by name or Altria's EIN.
  2. Review the plan's QDRO Approval Guidelines and Procedures available on the Fidelity platform.
  3. Obtain the participant's current account statement showing balance, loan balances, and account sources (pre-tax, Roth, employer contributions).
  4. Determine the division method, valuation date, and loan treatment consistent with the divorce settlement.
  5. Use the Fidelity QDRO Center drafting tool to generate the order, or draft manually using the plan's guidelines — understanding that manual drafts face a longer review window.
  6. Submit for court entry after review, then send the certified signed order to Fidelity for qualification.
  7. Follow up with Fidelity to confirm qualification and monitor the segregation of the alternate payee's share.

Common Mistakes

  • Naming the wrong plan: Using "Altria Deferred Profit-Sharing Plan" without specifying "Hourly" risks mismatch with the salaried plan.
  • Ignoring the loan balance: A significant participant loan can substantially affect net account value. Not addressing it creates implementation disputes.
  • Not using the Fidelity QDRO Center: Fidelity has a purpose-built tool for this plan. Using a generic template instead of the platform-generated order adds processing time and rejection risk.
  • Omitting gains and losses language: The plan's Master Trust investments produce ongoing gains and losses. The QDRO must say whether the alternate payee's share tracks those changes.
  • Confusing this with the salaried plan: Altria maintains a separate plan for salaried employees. The hourly and salaried plans are distinct ERISA plans with their own Form 5500 filings.

FAQ

Does this plan require a QDRO for divorce division?

Yes. It is a qualified ERISA plan. The plan cannot distribute benefits to an alternate payee without a QDRO accepted by Fidelity as the recordkeeper and plan administrator.

Who is the recordkeeper and how do I submit a QDRO?

Fidelity Investments. Submit through Fidelity's QDRO Center at qdro.fidelity.com. Using the plan-specific drafting tool on that platform reduces rejection risk and speeds up review.

How large is this plan?

Approximately $962 million in net assets as of December 31, 2024, per the plan's SEC Form 11-K. Approximately 1,644 active participants per the Form 5500.

Is this the same as the Altria salaried profit-sharing plan?

No. Altria maintains separate plans for hourly and salaried employees. They are distinct ERISA plans with different Form 5500 filings. A QDRO must name the correct plan.

What happens to an outstanding participant loan in the QDRO?

The QDRO should address it explicitly. Specify whether the alternate payee's percentage or dollar award is calculated before or after deducting the loan balance. Fidelity's QDRO Center will prompt for this choice when generating the order.
