McWane Salaried 401(k) in Divorce

The McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan is an individual-account defined contribution plan. The participant's balance is invested and fluctuates with market performance. In divorce, the account is typically divided by percentage or fixed dollar amount as of a specific date, adjusted for investment gains and losses until segregation.

The plan identification issue is the most critical drafting risk in a McWane case. McWane, Inc. maintains at least three separate retirement arrangements that appear in Form 5500 filings: the McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan, the McWane Hourly 401(k) Retirement Plan, and a McWane Salaried Pension Plan (defined benefit). A QDRO targeting the wrong plan will be rejected and require amendment. Confirm the correct plan from the participant's current benefits statement or pay stub before drafting.

The Form 5500 plan type codes for the salaried 401(k) include a safe harbor designation (2S), which typically means employer contributions vest immediately or on a short schedule as required under safe harbor rules. That can simplify vesting analysis — but the current plan documents should be confirmed before relying on that assumption.

QDRO Requirements

An order dividing the McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan should at minimum:

  • Identify the plan by its exact name: McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan — specifically salaried, not hourly, not pension.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient detail for Fidelity to locate the account.
  • State the exact amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event for calculation.
  • Address whether the assigned share is adjusted for investment gains and losses between the valuation date and segregation.
  • Avoid requiring any benefit type or form not offered under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage formula

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of a valuation date. Gains and losses apply from that date through segregation if the order says so. Clean and commonly accepted for defined contribution plans.

Fixed dollar formula

A specific dollar amount is awarded. The order must still specify whether that amount is adjusted for investment performance between the valuation date and the date Fidelity executes the transfer.

Safe harbor and vesting

The safe harbor designation in the plan type codes typically means employer safe harbor contributions vest on an accelerated schedule. Confirm current vesting terms from plan documents. The alternate payee's share should generally be drawn from the participant's vested balance.

Gains and losses

In a Fidelity-held investment account, the balance moves with market performance until the alternate payee's share is actually separated. Whether that movement benefits or reduces the alternate payee's share depends on how the order addresses it. Be explicit.

Steps to Divide

  1. Confirm the participant is enrolled in the McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan — not the McWane Hourly 401(k) and not the McWane Salaried Pension Plan.
  2. Obtain the current account statement showing all contribution sources: elective deferrals, Roth (if applicable), safe harbor match, and any profit sharing.
  3. Verify the participant's current vested balance and any vesting schedule for non-safe-harbor employer contributions.
  4. Decide on the division method: percentage or fixed dollar, with explicit gains and losses language.
  5. Use the Fidelity QDRO Center to generate plan-tailored language, or draft independently and submit for Fidelity review before court entry.
  6. After court entry, submit the certified order and follow through until Fidelity confirms the alternate payee account is established.

Common Mistakes

  • Targeting the wrong McWane plan: Submitting an order for the salaried plan when the participant is enrolled in the hourly plan — or vice versa — results in rejection. Confirm the plan name from the participant's benefits records.
  • Confusing the 401(k) with the pension: The McWane Salaried Pension Plan is a separate defined benefit arrangement and requires a different QDRO approach. Do not use a defined contribution order template to divide a defined benefit plan.
  • Omitting gains and losses language: A Fidelity account changes in value between drafting and segregation. Silence on investment performance creates avoidable disputes.
  • Using a generic order: Fidelity has plan-specific qualification requirements. A boilerplate order from another plan may not satisfy this plan's review criteria.

FAQ

Does the McWane Salaried 401(k) require a QDRO for divorce division?

Yes. An ERISA-governed 401(k) plan requires a qualified domestic relations order before the plan administrator will transfer benefits to an alternate payee. A divorce decree alone is not sufficient to direct Fidelity to create a separate account.

What is the difference between the McWane salaried and hourly 401(k) plans?

McWane, Inc. maintains separate plans for its salaried and hourly employees. The McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan covers salaried workers; the McWane Hourly 401(k) Retirement Plan covers hourly employees. These are distinct plans with separate plan numbers. A QDRO for one will not be accepted by the other.

Is the McWane Salaried 401(k) the same as the McWane pension plan?

No. The McWane Salaried Pension Plan is a separate defined benefit arrangement. A QDRO for the 401(k) addresses an individual investment account; a QDRO for the pension addresses a formula-based annuity. Different plans, different drafting requirements. Confirm which plan is at issue before drafting anything.

Who recordkeeps the McWane Salaried 401(k)?

Fidelity Investments is identified in secondary sources as the recordkeeper. The Fidelity QDRO Center at nb.fidelity.com/public/QDROFormGen/home offers plan-tailored QDRO language for Fidelity-recordkept plans. Contact McWane at 205-414-3100 to confirm current procedures.

Should gains and losses be included in the QDRO?

Yes. Because the account balance moves with market performance, the order should state whether the alternate payee's assigned share is adjusted for investment gains and losses from the valuation date through the date of actual segregation. Leaving it unstated creates uncertainty.
