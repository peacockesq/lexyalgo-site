401(k) in Divorce

The McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan is a defined contribution plan. The participant holds an individual account rather than a future pension benefit. In divorce, the account — or the portion that accrued during the marriage — can be divided through a Qualified Domestic Relations Order, which requires approval by the plan administrator before any distribution to an alternate payee.

For McWane cases, the most practical issue before drafting anything is confirming whether the participant is covered by the Salaried plan or the Hourly plan. Both plans share the same sponsor EIN (63-0139000) but have different plan numbers and different participant populations. The participant's most recent account statement or annual benefit notice should identify the specific plan. A QDRO that names the wrong plan will be rejected.

McWane, Inc. is a privately held company, which means no SEC Form 11-K filings are publicly available to confirm current plan features, contribution structures, or vesting schedules. Plan-specific details should be obtained directly from McWane HR or the plan administrator before finalizing any draft.

QDRO Requirements

A domestic relations order for the McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan must comply with ERISA, the Internal Revenue Code, and the plan's own qualification procedures. At minimum, the order should:

  • Identify the correct plan by exact name: McWane Salaried 401(k) Retirement Plan — not the Hourly plan.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient specificity for administrator review.
  • State the amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or triggering event.
  • Address whether the assigned share is adjusted for investment gains and losses after the valuation date.
  • Address the treatment of pre-tax and any Roth balances if both are present.
  • Address any outstanding loan balance.
  • Not require any benefit not available under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage formula

The most common approach for 401(k) plans: the alternate payee receives a specified percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of a valuation date. The order should state whether gains and losses from the valuation date to segregation are applied to that share.

Fixed dollar formula

A fixed dollar award works when the settlement is built around a specific amount. The order should still address gains and losses treatment — leaving it unstated is a common source of post-entry disputes.

Gains and losses

Between the valuation date and the date the alternate payee's share is actually segregated, the account balance can increase or decrease. The order should say explicitly whether the alternate payee's assigned amount tracks that investment experience or is frozen at the valuation date value.

Loan treatment

If the participant has an outstanding plan loan, the order should specify whether the alternate payee's percentage is applied to the gross balance or the net balance after deducting the loan. This is especially important if the loan balance is material.

Common Mistakes

  • Targeting the wrong plan: the most avoidable McWane-specific error is submitting a QDRO for the Salaried plan when the participant is in the Hourly plan, or vice versa. Always confirm plan identity from the participant's actual plan records.
  • Leaving gains and losses unstated: in any defined contribution plan, silence on investment experience between the valuation date and segregation creates disputes.
  • Skipping administrator verification: McWane is a private company; no public SPD is available to confirm current procedures. Direct contact before drafting is essential.
  • Vesting blindspot: employer contributions may be subject to a vesting schedule. An alternate payee's share of unvested employer contributions depends on both the settlement and plan rules.
  • Not addressing outstanding loans: if the participant account shows a loan balance, the order should explicitly address whether the alternate payee's share is gross or net of the loan.
