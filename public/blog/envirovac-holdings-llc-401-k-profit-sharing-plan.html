401(k) Profit Sharing Plan in Divorce

Envirovac's plan is a defined contribution plan, meaning the participant holds an individual account rather than a pension formula. The profit sharing component means the participant's balance may include employer-funded contributions beyond any 401(k) match — either tied to company performance or made at employer discretion. Those contributions are typically subject to vesting schedules.

In divorce, vesting matters. If the participant is not fully vested in the profit sharing portion at the time of division, the order should either address only the vested balance or specify how unvested amounts will be treated if they vest later. Most QDROs for defined contribution plans assign a percentage of the vested account as of a specific date, with gains and losses applied through segregation.

QDRO Requirements

A qualifying domestic relations order for the Envirovac Holdings 401(k) Profit Sharing Plan must satisfy ERISA's minimum content requirements and Lincoln Financial's review procedures. The order should:

  • Name the plan exactly — and confirm the current name with the plan administrator, given the potential sponsor change.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient specificity for administrator review.
  • State the dollar amount, percentage, or formula for the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event used for the calculation.
  • Address investment gains and losses from the valuation date through segregation or distribution.
  • Address vesting: specify whether the award is based on the vested account only or includes unvested amounts contingent on future vesting.
  • Address pre-tax and Roth source treatment if both exist in the account.
  • Address outstanding loan balance treatment if a loan is reflected on the account.

Division Formulas

Percentage of vested account

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's total vested account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for gains and losses until segregation. This is the most common approach and covers all contribution sources proportionally.

Fixed dollar amount

A specific dollar amount can work when the settlement uses a hard number. The order should address whether gains and losses apply after the valuation date. A frozen fixed award can create a dispute if the account value drops before segregation.

Vesting treatment

If the participant has unvested profit sharing contributions, the order can cover only the vested balance as of the valuation date, or it can include a provision for the alternate payee to receive a proportional share of any amounts that vest later. The settlement agreement should drive this choice.

Loan balance treatment

If the participant has an outstanding 401(k) loan, state whether the alternate payee's share is calculated from the gross balance before subtracting the loan, or from the net balance after. This is a common ambiguity that produces avoidable disputes.

Common Mistakes

  • Wrong sponsor name: if the plan sponsor changed to Enviro Hydro Holdings, Inc. in 2024, using the old Envirovac Holdings, LLC name without verification could cause a plan identification problem. Always confirm the current plan name.
  • Ignoring unvested profit sharing: for plans with a profit sharing component, failing to address unvested amounts can leave the alternate payee's interest undefined if the participant vests after the divorce is final.
  • No gains and losses language: defined contribution accounts fluctuate with market performance. Without explicit language, the award value is uncertain between the valuation date and segregation.
  • Overlooking the loan: if the participant has an outstanding 401(k) loan, the order should address how it affects the alternate payee's share calculation.
  • Not requesting Lincoln Financial procedures: Lincoln has its own QDRO sample language and review process. Working with those materials upfront avoids rejection after court entry.
