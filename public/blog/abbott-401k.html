Abbott Laboratories 401(k) Plan: Stock Retirement Plan (SRP)

If a divorce involves Abbott Laboratories retirement benefits, the first drafting issue is making sure the order targets the right plan. Abbott's Stock Retirement Plan (SRP) is the company's 401(k) defined contribution plan, while the Abbott Annuity Retirement Plan (ARP) is a separate pension. A clean QDRO for the SRP should identify the correct plan, address pre-tax and Roth balances, and state how gains or losses are handled after the valuation date.

401(k) in Divorce

The Abbott SRP is a defined contribution plan, which means the participant has an individual account rather than a pension formula. In divorce, that matters because the account can usually be divided by a fixed dollar amount, a percentage, or a formula tied to a valuation date. Unlike a pension annuity, the account balance can rise or fall with market performance between the date used for division and the date the alternate payee's share is actually segregated or distributed.

For Abbott cases, the practical issue is not just how much the alternate payee receives. The order should also say what account sources are being divided. If the participant has both pre-tax and Roth money in the SRP, the order should make clear whether each source is shared proportionally or handled under a different specified method. Without that detail, implementation questions can surface after entry.

QDRO Requirements

A domestic relations order for the Abbott SRP should be written as a plan-specific implementation document, not just a general divorce provision. At minimum, it should:

  • Identify the correct plan by name: Abbott Stock Retirement Plan (SRP).
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient specificity for administrator review.
  • State the exact amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event used for calculation.
  • Address whether the assigned share is adjusted for investment gains and losses after the valuation date.
  • Clarify treatment of pre-tax and Roth balances so source-level administration is not left to guesswork.
  • Address any outstanding loan issue if the participant account shows an SRP loan balance.
  • Avoid requiring any form or benefit not available under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage formula

A percentage award can work well when the parties want the alternate payee to receive a set share of the account as of a certain date, with gains and losses thereafter. Example structure: the alternate payee receives 50% of the participant's vested SRP account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for subsequent investment gains or losses until segregation.

Fixed dollar formula

A fixed dollar award may be useful when the settlement uses a hard number. If this method is used, the order should still say whether gains and losses apply after the valuation date. Leaving that point unstated can create an avoidable dispute.

Source-based allocation

Where the account includes both pre-tax and Roth money, the order can say that each source is divided pro rata in the same percentage as the total assigned share. This is often cleaner than dividing one source first and leaving the rest implied.

Gains and losses

For a defined contribution plan like the SRP, gains and losses language matters. A precise order states whether the alternate payee's assigned amount is adjusted for all investment experience from the valuation date through the date of segregation, or whether the award is frozen.

Steps

  1. Confirm the exact plan from the participant's current statement or benefits records: Abbott Stock Retirement Plan (SRP).
  2. Confirm you are not dealing with the separate Abbott Annuity Retirement Plan (ARP) pension.
  3. Gather the latest account information, including any pre-tax, Roth, and loan details shown on plan records.
  4. Choose the division method: percentage, fixed dollar amount, or another clearly stated formula.
  5. Draft gains and losses language that fits the settlement.
  6. Send the draft for review through the Abbott Benefits Center or the plan's current QDRO review channel before submitting it for signature.
  7. After court entry, submit the certified signed order with any required supporting paperwork and follow through until qualification is confirmed.

Common Mistakes

  • Mixing up SRP and ARP: this is one of the biggest avoidable Abbott-specific errors. The SRP is the 401(k); the ARP is a different pension plan.
  • Leaving gains and losses unstated: in a moving investment account, silence can create conflict.
  • Ignoring Roth treatment: if Roth money exists in the account, the order should say how it is divided instead of assuming the administrator will infer it.
  • Using a generic order: boilerplate language that works for one employer plan may not map cleanly to the Abbott SRP.
  • Skipping draft review: when no public model order is available, administrator review before entry can save time and amendments later.

FAQ

Does the Abbott Laboratories 401(k) plan usually require a QDRO for divorce division?

Yes. A divorce judgment alone is usually not enough to divide an employer 401(k) plan. The Abbott SRP generally requires a qualifying domestic relations order before the alternate payee can receive plan benefits.

What is the difference between Abbott's SRP and ARP?

The SRP is Abbott's Stock Retirement Plan, which is the company's 401(k) defined contribution plan. The ARP is the Abbott Annuity Retirement Plan, which is a defined benefit pension. A QDRO should identify the correct one by exact name.

Should pre-tax and Roth balances both be addressed in the order?

Yes. If the participant account includes both sources, the order should clearly state how each is divided. A pro rata allocation across sources is often cleaner than leaving that issue unresolved.

Why do gains and losses matter so much for the SRP?

Because the SRP is a defined contribution account invested over time. The balance can change after the valuation date, so the order should say whether the alternate payee's share tracks those changes until segregation or distribution.

Where can I get Abbott's current QDRO procedures?

Start with the Abbott Benefits Center at 844-306-9222. No official public model QDRO for the SRP was identified in the research used for this page, so direct confirmation of current procedures is important.
