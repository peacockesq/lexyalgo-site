In Divorce

A lot of people get sloppy here because they hear “403(b)” and assume different rules. Not this one. An ERISA-governed 403(b) plan with employer involvement is divided in divorce through a qualified domestic relations order just like a 401(k). A decree by itself is not enough for the plan administrator to carve out benefits to an alternate payee.

Like other defined contribution plans, this one is usually divided by assigning a percentage or fixed dollar amount of the participant account as of a stated valuation date. Because the account is participant-directed, investment gains and losses after that date should be addressed in the order. If the account contains multiple investment contracts or custodial holdings, the formula still needs to be simple enough for the administrator to implement without guesswork.

QDRO Requirements

A qualifying domestic relations order for this plan should do the following:

  • Name the plan exactly: SANTA ROSA COMMUNITY HEALTH CENTERS 403(B) PLAN.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee clearly enough for administrator review.
  • State the amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event used for the calculation.
  • Address gains and losses from the valuation date through segregation or distribution.
  • Address all intended account sources clearly, including Roth or employer contribution sources if applicable.
  • Address any outstanding participant loan if one exists.
  • Avoid requiring any form of benefit or distribution the plan does not permit.
Drafting note: This page treats the plan as ERISA-covered based on the plan type information provided. That means the order should be drafted to satisfy ERISA section 414(p) and the plan's own review procedures, even though the plan is labeled 403(b).

Division Formulas

Percentage award

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for gains and losses through segregation. This is the usual approach for a defined contribution 403(b).

Fixed dollar award

A hard dollar number can work, but the order should say whether that number is adjusted for gains and losses between valuation and transfer.

Investment experience

Because this is a participant-directed account, gains and losses language is not optional if you want the award implemented cleanly.

ERISA compliance

The order should be written as a QDRO meeting ERISA section 414(p), because an ERISA-governed 403(b) is processed the same basic way as an ERISA 401(k) for divorce division purposes.

Steps to Divide

  1. Confirm the exact plan name from the participant's benefit materials: SANTA ROSA COMMUNITY HEALTH CENTERS 403(B) PLAN.
  2. Obtain the current statement and identify the balance or valuation date used in the marital settlement.
  3. Confirm the current administrator or recordkeeper and request the plan's QDRO procedures.
  4. Draft the order as a QDRO compliant with ERISA section 414(p), even though the plan is a 403(b).
  5. State the award formula clearly and include gains-and-losses language.
  6. Use administrator pre-review before court entry whenever possible.
  7. After entry, submit the certified order and monitor the transfer until the alternate payee's share is actually segregated or paid.

Common Mistakes

  • Assuming a 403(b) does not need a QDRO when the plan is ERISA-covered.
  • Using generic decree language instead of a formal QDRO framework.
  • Leaving gains and losses unstated on a participant-directed account.
  • Failing to confirm current administrator procedures when no public recordkeeper was verified.
  • Using an abbreviated or informal plan name instead of the exact Form 5500 name.

FAQ

Does this 403(b) require a QDRO in divorce?

Yes. Because this plan is treated here as ERISA-covered, division requires a qualified domestic relations order under ERISA section 414(p), just like an ERISA 401(k).

Is a 403(b) divided differently from a 401(k)?

Not in the basic sense when the 403(b) is ERISA-governed. The order still needs the same core elements: exact plan identification, a clear award formula, and language addressing implementation issues like gains and losses.

Was a public recordkeeper confirmed?

No. The research reviewed did not confirm a current public recordkeeper or public QDRO portal for this plan.

Why does gains-and-losses language matter?

Because the account balance can move after the valuation date. Clear language avoids disputes over whether the alternate payee shares in market changes before transfer.

Was an official model QDRO found?

No official public model QDRO was identified in the research reviewed for this page.
