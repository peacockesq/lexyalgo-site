Harlem Children's Zone Profit Sharing Plan in Divorce

The Harlem Children's Zone, Inc. Profit Sharing Plan is a defined contribution plan, meaning each eligible employee holds an individual account rather than an entitlement to a future pension payment. In divorce, the plan account is divided by a QDRO that assigns a percentage or dollar amount to the alternate payee as of a specific date, with explicit language on how investment gains and losses between the valuation date and the segregation date are handled.

The plan is labeled a "profit sharing plan" rather than a "401(k) plan," which reflects the structure of employer contributions. In a profit sharing plan, the employer (HCZ, in this case) contributes a discretionary amount to eligible employees' accounts rather than matching employee deferrals dollar for dollar. Employees may also make elective deferrals, depending on how the plan is designed. The Form 5500 plan type codes (2E, 2F, 2G) indicate that both employee and employer contributions are features of the plan.

Because HCZ is a nonprofit, it is worth confirming that this plan is not a 403(b) arrangement, which would require a different type of domestic relations order in some cases. The Form 5500 plan type codes do not include the 403(b) designation, and the plan is reported as an ERISA profit sharing plan — so a QDRO is the correct vehicle. That said, confirm with the plan sponsor before drafting.

The plan's recordkeeper was not identified in publicly available research. Contacting HCZ's HR department directly is the most reliable path to current procedures.

QDRO Requirements

An order dividing the Harlem Children's Zone, Inc. Profit Sharing Plan should at minimum:

  • Identify the plan by its exact name: Harlem Children's Zone, Inc. Profit Sharing Plan.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient detail for the plan administrator to locate the account.
  • State the exact amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event for the calculation.
  • Address whether the assigned share is adjusted for investment gains and losses between the valuation date and actual segregation.
  • Address vesting — confirm that the amounts assigned to the alternate payee are drawn from vested balances.
  • Avoid requiring any benefit type or form not offered under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage formula

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of a specific date. Gains and losses from that date through segregation should be addressed explicitly. Clean and commonly used for defined contribution profit sharing plans.

Fixed dollar formula

A specific dollar amount is awarded to the alternate payee. The order should still address investment performance between valuation and segregation — a frozen amount and a gains-adjusted amount can produce very different outcomes if processing takes months.

Vesting and profit sharing

Profit sharing contributions may vest on a schedule separate from elective deferrals. In a nonprofit plan without a standard safe harbor election, vesting could span several years. Confirm the participant's current vested balance before drafting to avoid assigning an amount that includes unvested employer contributions.

Gains and losses

Because the account is invested, its value changes from the valuation date through the date the alternate payee's share is actually transferred. The order must say explicitly whether that change benefits or does not apply to the alternate payee — silence creates disputes at implementation.

  • Ignoring vesting: Profit sharing contributions may not be fully vested. Assigning unvested amounts creates implementation complications if the participant later forfeits those funds.
  • Omitting gains and losses language: Silence on investment performance between valuation and segregation is avoidable and commonly leads to disputes at implementation.

FAQ

Does the Harlem Children's Zone Profit Sharing Plan require a QDRO for divorce division?

Yes. This is an ERISA-governed defined contribution plan. A qualified domestic relations order is required before the plan will transfer any portion of the participant's account to an alternate payee. A New York divorce judgment does not, by itself, direct the plan to make that transfer.

Is a profit sharing plan divided the same way as a 401(k) in divorce?

The QDRO mechanics are similar — both are defined contribution plans and both require an order specifying a percentage or fixed dollar amount, adjusted for gains and losses. The main practical difference is that profit sharing employer contributions sometimes vest on a longer schedule. Confirm the participant's vested balance before drafting.

Is this plan a 403(b) because HCZ is a nonprofit?

Based on Form 5500, this plan is reported as an ERISA profit sharing plan — not a 403(b). The two are different plan types with different rules and different order requirements. That said, confirm with HCZ directly before drafting to make sure you are working with current plan information.

Who recordkeeps the Harlem Children's Zone Profit Sharing Plan?

The recordkeeper was not confirmed in publicly available research. Contact HCZ HR directly at 212-234-6200 to identify the current plan administrator or recordkeeper and request current QDRO procedures.

Should gains and losses be included in the QDRO?

Yes. In a defined contribution account, the balance moves between the valuation date and the date the funds are actually transferred to the alternate payee. The order should explicitly state whether those changes affect the alternate payee's share.
