Strand Associates Profit Sharing Trust: QDRO Guide for Divorce

The STRAND ASSOCIATES, INC. EMPLOYEES PROFIT SHARING TRUST is an ERISA-covered defined contribution profit-sharing plan. In divorce, it is divided by qualified domestic relations order, not by decree language alone. The core drafting issues are exact plan identification, the valuation date, and whether the alternate payee shares post-valuation gains and losses.

In Divorce

This plan is not a pension with a monthly benefit formula. It is an ERISA-covered profit-sharing trust, which means the participant has an individual account balance. That makes it a defined contribution plan for QDRO purposes, and it can be divided by assigning the alternate payee a percentage or stated dollar amount of the account as of a specific date.

The drafting risk is not complexity so much as sloppiness. The order needs the exact plan name, a clean calculation formula, and gains-and-losses language. If the participant had employer contributions, rollover money, or multiple investment funds in the account, the order should still be written so the administrator can apply one precise formula across the whole vested balance.

QDRO Requirements

A qualifying domestic relations order for this plan should do the following:

  • Name the plan exactly: STRAND ASSOCIATES, INC. EMPLOYEES PROFIT SHARING TRUST.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee clearly enough for administrator review.
  • State the amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event used for the calculation.
  • Address gains and losses from the valuation date through segregation or distribution.
  • Address all intended account sources clearly, including Roth or employer contribution sources if applicable.
  • Address any outstanding participant loan if one exists.
  • Avoid requiring any form of benefit or distribution the plan does not permit.
Drafting note: Because no confirmed public recordkeeper or model order was located, pre-approval with the plan administrator is the smart move here. Generic 401(k) language often works badly when a profit-sharing trust has its own internal review checklist.

Division Formulas

Percentage award

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for gains and losses until segregation. This is usually the cleanest formula for a defined contribution profit-sharing trust.

Fixed dollar award

A stated dollar amount can work if the settlement already fixes a number. The order should still say whether that amount is adjusted for investment performance after the valuation date.

Gains and losses

Because the account balance moves with contributions, distributions, and investment performance, the order should say whether the awarded share tracks gains and losses from the valuation date through the date of transfer.

Vested balance focus

If the plan distinguishes between vested and non-vested amounts, the order should make clear whether the award applies to the vested account balance only. Most divorce divisions should not try to assign benefits the participant has not vested in.

Steps to Divide

  1. Confirm the exact plan name from the participant statement or current plan materials: STRAND ASSOCIATES, INC. EMPLOYEES PROFIT SHARING TRUST.
  2. Obtain the latest account statement and identify the balance to be divided, including whether the settlement uses a valuation date.
  3. Contact the plan administrator to confirm current QDRO procedures and whether the plan uses an internal review form or preferred order language.
  4. Choose the award structure: percentage, fixed dollar amount, or another precise formula tied to the account balance.
  5. Draft explicit gains-and-losses language and make clear the award applies to the participant's vested defined contribution account.
  6. Submit the draft for administrator review before court entry when possible.
  7. After entry, send the certified signed order and follow through until the transfer is completed.

Common Mistakes

  • Using the wrong plan name or shortening it informally instead of using the exact Form 5500 name.
  • Treating the plan like a pension instead of a defined contribution account.
  • Leaving gains and losses unstated.
  • Failing to confirm current administrator procedures when no public model order was found.
  • Trying to assign benefits beyond what the plan can actually pay under its terms.

FAQ

Does dividing the Strand Associates profit-sharing trust require a QDRO?

Yes. Because this is an ERISA-covered employer retirement plan, a domestic relations order must qualify under federal law before the plan can transfer benefits to an alternate payee.

Is this plan a pension or a defined contribution account?

It is a defined contribution profit-sharing trust. The participant has an account balance rather than a monthly annuity formula, so division is usually done with a percentage or dollar-based account award.

Was a public recordkeeper confirmed for this plan?

No. The research located third-party QDRO directory references to the plan, but no official source confirming the current recordkeeper or a public QDRO portal.

Why does gains and losses language matter here?

Because the account balance changes with market performance and plan activity. Without gains-and-losses language, the parties can end up fighting over timing drift between the valuation date and actual transfer.
