401(k) in Divorce

A 401(k) plan is divided differently from a pension because the asset is an individual account rather than a monthly future benefit. The core drafting issue is usually how much of the participant's account is assigned to the alternate payee and how market movement is handled before distribution.

Because this plan appears to include multiple contribution sources, including pre-tax and Roth money, the order should be specific enough to avoid disputes about what is and is not included in the assigned share.

For defined contribution plans, vague settlement language can create avoidable review delays. Precision matters.

QDRO Requirements

A domestic relations order typically must identify the participant, the alternate payee, the plan, and the amount or percentage to be awarded. It also should avoid directing any form of benefit not available under the plan.

  • Correct plan name and party identification
  • Clear award formula or fixed dollar amount
  • Direction on gains and losses, if intended
  • Treatment of loan balances, if relevant to the case
  • Submission in the format and channel currently required by the plan administrator

Division Formulas

For a 401(k) account, the two most common approaches are a percentage award and a fixed dollar award. The best fit depends on the settlement terms, timing, and whether the parties want market movement after the valuation date to be shared.

Percentage Formula

This approach awards the alternate payee a stated percentage of the participant's account or a stated percentage of a defined portion of the account as of a specified date.

  • Often easier to align with investment performance
  • Can reduce disputes if gains and losses are addressed clearly
  • Useful where the account remains invested during review

Fixed Dollar Formula

This approach awards a stated dollar amount from the participant's account as of a stated date, with explicit language on whether post-valuation gains or losses apply.

  • Can be straightforward in settlement drafting
  • Needs careful language if processing is delayed
  • Should state whether investment change is included or excluded

Steps

  1. Confirm the exact legal plan name and current QDRO submission contact.
  2. Review the settlement terms and determine whether the award is percentage-based or fixed-dollar.
  3. Draft the order to address all intended contribution sources, including pre-tax and Roth balances if applicable.
  4. State the valuation date and whether gains and losses apply through segregation or distribution.
  5. Submit the proposed order for plan review and revise if the administrator requests changes.
  6. Obtain court entry and submit the signed order in the form required for qualification.

Common Mistakes

  • Using the wrong plan name or outdated administrator details
  • Failing to specify whether the award covers Roth and pre-tax sources
  • Leaving gains and losses ambiguous
  • Ignoring possible account restrictions during review
  • Assuming a public model order exists when no official public model has been identified
If the participant expects to move money, take a loan, or rebalance assets, possible review holds can become a timing issue in the case.

FAQ

Does this plan require a QDRO for divorce division?

Yes, in most divorce-related divisions involving a 401(k), the plan administrator needs a domestic relations order that is reviewed and accepted as qualified before payment can be made to an alternate payee.

Why does it matter that the plan has Roth and pre-tax contributions?

Those sources can have different tax characteristics. Drafting should make clear whether the award applies across all sources or only certain subaccounts so the administrator can implement the order as intended.

What if there is no public model QDRO for this plan?

That usually means the order needs to be drafted more carefully around general qualification rules and any current administrator guidance. It also makes pre-approval review more valuable.

Can the account be frozen while the order is being reviewed?

Public research suggests that it can. Parties should not assume unrestricted access remains available during the review process.

Does immediate vesting matter in the division analysis?

Yes. Immediate vesting means the relevant vested balances may be simpler to identify because employee contributions and company match are reportedly already vested, reducing one common source of confusion.
