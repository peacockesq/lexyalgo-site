Dividing This Plan in Divorce

The DRG 401(k) is a defined contribution plan. Participants hold individual account balances representing their own contributions, any employer match, and investment performance. There is no formula-based pension annuity — the account balance is what gets divided.

The Marital Portion

The portion of the 401(k) account that accrued during the marriage is generally marital property subject to division. If the participant contributed to the plan before the marriage, those premarital contributions and the investment growth attributable to them may be separate property under state law. Tracing premarital balances requires historical account records, which the plan recordkeeper or USI can help obtain.

Vesting

Employer contributions to a 401(k) vest over time according to the plan's vesting schedule. Unvested employer contributions are not yet fully owned by the participant. The QDRO should address whether the alternate payee's share is calculated based on the vested balance only, or whether unvested employer contributions are included (which is state-law dependent). Confirm the vesting schedule in the plan's SPD.

Coverture fractions and 401(k) plans: A coverture fraction is an analytical tool used to calculate the marital share of an account — it is not a distribution method. The QDRO specifies the award (a percentage, dollar amount, or formula). The plan administrator then applies the QDRO to determine the alternate payee's actual benefit. Parties should not confuse using a coverture analysis with how the plan actually pays out.

QDRO Requirements

Plan-specific requirements — including any administrator preferences for format, calculation language, or submission procedures — should be obtained from USI Insurance Services or DRG's HR benefits team before finalizing the draft.

At minimum, the QDRO must:

  • Identify the plan by exact name: Diversified Restaurant Group, LLC 401(k) Plan.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee by full legal name and last known mailing address.
  • State the amount or percentage of the benefit being assigned, or the formula used to determine it.
  • Specify the valuation date or triggering event.
  • Address investment gains and losses after the valuation date.
  • Address the vested balance versus total balance if the participant's employer contributions are not fully vested.
  • Address outstanding loan balances if applicable.
  • Not require any benefit or form of payment not available under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage of vested balance

The most common approach. The alternate payee receives a set percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for investment gains and losses until the alternate payee's account is segregated. Simple, clean, and widely accepted by 401(k) plan administrators.

Fixed dollar award

A specific dollar amount as of the valuation date. Should explicitly state whether that amount is adjusted for gains and losses after the valuation date. In an account with ongoing investment activity, silence on this point creates disputes.

Marital share formula

For participants with a premarital balance, the QDRO can award a percentage of the marital portion only — i.e., contributions and growth during the marriage. This requires historical balance data from the plan recordkeeper or USI. Agree on the formula and data source before drafting.

Gains and losses clause

Standard language for a defined contribution plan. Specifies that the alternate payee's assigned share grows or shrinks proportionately with the account's investment performance from the valuation date until the date of actual segregation or distribution. This protects both parties from timing-based inequity.

Common Mistakes

  • Not contacting USI or DRG benefits before drafting: The plan has a named benefits partner with public contact info. Use it. Drafting without the SPD or plan procedures wastes time.
  • Omitting gains and losses language: A 401(k) balance moves with market performance. The QDRO must be explicit about whether the alternate payee's share adjusts for investment activity after the valuation date.
  • Treating unvested employer contributions as divisible without confirmation: Restaurant 401(k) plans often use graded or cliff vesting for employer matches. Claiming unvested employer contributions without confirming state law and plan rules is a drafting error.
  • Using a generic template: While most 401(k) plans follow ERISA's minimum requirements, plan-specific administrative procedures can add requirements. A generic template that does not reflect this plan's requirements may be rejected.
  • Confusing the benefits broker (USI) with the recordkeeper: USI is the benefits partner/broker, not necessarily the investment recordkeeper. The plan may use a separate financial institution (e.g., Fidelity, Vanguard, Principal, etc.) to hold and manage plan assets. Confirm the recordkeeper identity before submitting the QDRO.

FAQ

Does the DRG 401(k) require a QDRO?

Yes. It is a qualified ERISA plan. A divorce judgment does not authorize the plan to distribute benefits to an alternate payee without a QDRO accepted by the administrator.

Who is the benefits contact for this plan?

USI Insurance Services at 888-336-7463 or brcca@usi.com is DRG's benefits partner. DRG's internal HR benefits email is benefits@drgfood.com. The DRG Restaurant Support Center is at 702-880-5818, ext. 9591.

What brands does DRG operate?

Taco Bell, Arby's, and other restaurant brands, operating from a Las Vegas-area headquarters.

Is USI the recordkeeper for the 401(k) accounts?

Not necessarily. USI is the benefits broker/partner. The actual investment recordkeeper for plan accounts (the entity that holds participant funds) may be a separate financial institution. Confirm the recordkeeper identity when contacting DRG or USI.

Can the alternate payee roll over their distribution?

Generally yes. QDRO-approved distributions from a defined contribution plan to an alternate payee can typically be rolled over to an IRA tax-free. The alternate payee should consult a tax advisor and confirm rollover rules with the plan administrator before electing a distribution method.
