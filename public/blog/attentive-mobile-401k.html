401(k) in Divorce

The Attentive Mobile 401(k) Plan is a defined contribution plan. The participant's account — not a future monthly benefit — is the asset. In divorce, the portion of the account that accrued during the marriage may be divisible through a Qualified Domestic Relations Order. Because defined contribution accounts are invested and their value changes over time, the domestic relations order must address what happens to the assigned share between the valuation date and the date of actual account segregation.

Attentive's plan supports both pre-tax and Roth (after-tax) contributions, confirmed by the plan type code in the Form 5500 filing (code 2R indicating Roth). If the participant's account holds balances in both pre-tax and Roth sources, the QDRO should clearly state whether each source is divided proportionally or under a different specified method. Without that specificity, the administrator is left to make assumptions, which can create complications during implementation.

The company's employer match — dollar-for-dollar up to $1,500 annually — may be subject to a vesting schedule. An alternate payee's share of unvested employer contributions depends on both the plan's vesting rules and the terms of the divorce settlement. Confirm vesting details before finalizing the order.

QDRO Requirements

A domestic relations order submitted for the Attentive Mobile 401(k) Plan must comply with ERISA, the Internal Revenue Code, and the plan's Fidelity-administered qualification procedures. At minimum, the order should:

  • Identify the plan by name: Attentive Mobile 401(k) Plan.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient specificity for administrator review.
  • State the amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or triggering event.
  • Address whether the assigned share is adjusted for gains and losses after the valuation date and until segregation.
  • Address the treatment of pre-tax and Roth balances separately, if both exist in the account.
  • Address any outstanding loan balance.
  • Not require any benefit not available under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage formula

Assigning the alternate payee a percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of a specified date is the most common approach. The order should state whether that percentage is adjusted for investment performance between the valuation date and the date of segregation.

Fixed dollar formula

A fixed dollar award works when the parties want certainty around the amount. The order should still address gains and losses from the valuation date forward to avoid post-entry disputes.

Roth and pre-tax treatment

If the participant has contributed to both pre-tax and Roth sources, the order should specify how each is divided. A pro rata approach — where the alternate payee's stated percentage applies to each source — is often the cleanest method. Alternatively, the order can specify one source type to be used first.

Employer match considerations

Attentive's employer match (dollar-for-dollar, up to $1,500/year) may be vested differently from employee contributions. The order should address whether the alternate payee's share includes unvested employer contributions and, if so, how vesting is handled at distribution.

Common Mistakes

  • Leaving gains and losses unstated: in an invested account, silence on this point creates disputes. The order should explicitly address what happens to the alternate payee's share between the valuation date and segregation.
  • Ignoring Roth balances: Attentive's plan supports Roth contributions. If Roth money is in the account, the order should address it explicitly — not leave it for the administrator to figure out.
  • Vesting blindspot on employer match: Attentive's dollar-for-dollar match is capped at $1,500/year, but the vesting schedule matters. Confirm current vesting rules before finalizing the award.
  • Not addressing outstanding loans: if the participant has an outstanding plan loan, the order should specify whether the alternate payee's share is calculated on the gross or net balance.
  • Using a generic template without Fidelity pre-review: Fidelity administers plan-specific QDRO requirements. A generic 401(k) order may not satisfy Attentive-specific procedures. Pre-review through qdro.fidelity.com is the most reliable safeguard.

FAQ

Does the Attentive Mobile 401(k) require a QDRO to divide in divorce?

Yes. As an ERISA-governed defined contribution plan, a qualifying domestic relations order reviewed by the plan administrator is required before any distribution to an alternate payee. A divorce judgment alone does not transfer retirement plan benefits.

Who processes QDROs for the Attentive Mobile plan?

Fidelity Investments serves as the plan's recordkeeper and processes QDRO review. Use the Fidelity QDRO Center at qdro.fidelity.com for plan-specific guidelines and model language. The Fidelity Benefits Service Center is also reachable at 1-800-343-0860.

Can both pre-tax and Roth balances be divided in a QDRO?

Yes. The Attentive Mobile 401(k) Plan supports both pre-tax and Roth contributions. If the participant has both source types, the QDRO should explicitly state how each is divided — for example, pro rata across all sources, or by specifying which source is used first.

How does Attentive's employer match factor into a QDRO?

Attentive offers a dollar-for-dollar match up to $1,500 per year. Employer contributions may be subject to a vesting schedule. An alternate payee may be entitled to a share of unvested match contributions depending on the settlement and plan rules. Confirm current vesting terms with the plan administrator.

Where can I get Attentive's current QDRO procedures?
