401(k) and Incentive Plan in Divorce

The Clearing House Incentive Account Plan is a defined contribution plan, meaning participants hold individual accounts rather than a pension formula. The "Incentive Account" label is significant: it signals that employer-funded incentive or profit-sharing contributions — not just employee salary deferrals — are a core feature of the plan. In divorce, that distinction can matter when calculating the marital share.

If only the marital portion of the account is subject to division, the QDRO formula should define the scope clearly — for example, by specifying a valuation date that corresponds to the date of marriage or date of separation. If the parties intend to divide the full vested account balance, the formula should say so. Either way, gains and losses language is important because account values shift between the valuation date and the date the alternate payee's account is actually segregated.

QDRO Requirements

A qualifying domestic relations order for this plan must satisfy ERISA's minimum content requirements and any plan-specific procedures. The order should:

  • Name the plan exactly: The Clearing House Incentive Account Plan.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with enough specificity for administrator review.
  • State the dollar amount, percentage, or formula for the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event used to calculate the share.
  • Address investment gains and losses from valuation through segregation or distribution.
  • Address pre-tax and Roth source treatment if both exist in the account.
  • Avoid requiring any type or form of benefit not otherwise provided under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage of total vested account

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's total vested account balance — including both employee deferrals and employer incentive contributions — as of the valuation date, adjusted for gains and losses until segregation. This is the most common approach for defined contribution plans.

Fixed dollar amount

A specific dollar amount can work when the settlement uses a hard number. Address whether gains and losses apply between the valuation date and segregation. Without that language, a fixed dollar award leaves timing risk unresolved.

Marital share only

If only the portion of the account accumulated during the marriage is subject to division, the order should define the calculation period (typically date of marriage through date of separation or the valuation date) and address how the account balance is allocated to that period.

Pre-tax and Roth sources

If the participant holds both pre-tax and Roth contributions, specify how each source is divided. A pro rata allocation — the alternate payee receives the same percentage of each source as of the total — is typically the cleanest approach.

Steps to Divide

  1. Confirm the exact plan name from the participant's benefit statement: The Clearing House Incentive Account Plan.
  2. Obtain the participant's current account statement showing all contribution sources (pre-tax, Roth, employer incentive) and any outstanding loan balance.
  3. Confirm the current recordkeeper and QDRO submission procedures — contact The Clearing House HR or check qdro.fidelity.com if Fidelity is confirmed.
  4. Choose the division scope and formula: full vested account or marital portion only; percentage or fixed amount.
  5. Draft the order with explicit gains/losses language and source treatment.
  6. Submit for plan administrator pre-review before court entry when possible.
  7. After court entry, submit the certified signed order and follow through until the alternate payee's separate account is confirmed.

Common Mistakes

  • Wrong plan name: using a shorthand like "The Clearing House 401(k)" instead of the exact plan name — The Clearing House Incentive Account Plan — can cause rejection.
  • Forgetting employer incentive contributions: if the settlement intends to divide the full account, the formula should cover all sources including employer incentive balances, not just employee deferrals.
  • No gains and losses language: participant-directed investment accounts change in value daily. Without gains/losses language, the awarded amount is uncertain between the valuation date and segregation.
  • Ignoring Roth balances: if both pre-tax and Roth money exists, the order should specify which sources are being divided to avoid implementation disputes.
  • Skipping pre-review: sending a draft to the plan administrator before court entry can prevent costly rejections and amendments after entry.

FAQ

Does dividing this plan in divorce require a QDRO?

Yes. The Clearing House Incentive Account Plan is an ERISA-governed retirement plan. A divorce judgment alone cannot direct the plan to pay benefits to an alternate payee. A qualifying domestic relations order is required.

What does "Incentive Account" mean for the QDRO?

It signals that employer-funded incentive and profit-sharing contributions are a core plan feature. The QDRO formula should clearly address whether those employer contributions are included in the division — both parties should agree on this before drafting.

Who processes QDROs for this plan?

Third-party sources report Fidelity as the recordkeeper. If confirmed, the Fidelity QDRO Center (qdro.fidelity.com) is the starting point for plan-specific DRO Approval Guidelines. Verify current procedures directly with the plan administrator.

Should gains and losses be addressed?

Yes. Because this is a participant-directed defined contribution account, the balance changes over time. Gains and losses language clarifies whether the alternate payee's share tracks investment performance from the valuation date through segregation.

Where can I get the current QDRO procedures?

Contact The Clearing House Payments Company HR directly or check the Fidelity QDRO Center (qdro.fidelity.com). No official plan-issued model QDRO was identified in the research for this page.
