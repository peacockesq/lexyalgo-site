How this plan works in divorce

Sifive, Inc. 401(k) Plan is treated here as a defined contribution plan. In plain English: the divorce order usually awards a percentage or fixed amount from an account, then the administrator implements that award under the plan's current QDRO procedures.

This page does not assume any special plan feature beyond what the Form 5500 supports. Whether the account includes pre-tax money, Roth money, employer match, loans, or multiple sources should be confirmed with the administrator before drafting. And to be crystal clear: for 401(k)-style plans, a coverture analysis may matter in the property case, but the QDRO itself still needs to state the award cleanly rather than pretending coverture is the payment method.

Defined contribution division points to address

Percentage award

A percentage-based award is common when the settlement gives the alternate payee a share of the account as of a stated valuation date.

Fixed dollar award

A fixed amount can work too, but the order should still say whether gains and losses apply after the valuation date.

Investment gains and losses

Because the balance can move with the market, the order should state whether the awarded share tracks post-valuation gains and losses until segregation.

Loans and source balances

If the plan account includes loans, Roth sources, or separate subaccounts, those details should be confirmed before filing.

Common defined contribution mistakes

  • Using the wrong plan name in the order.
  • Leaving gains and losses unstated.
  • Ignoring loans or source-level account detail.
  • Assuming old administrator instructions are still current.
