401(k) in Divorce

The FST Technical Services 401(k) Plan is a defined contribution account, meaning each participant holds an individual balance that can be divided in divorce. The plan includes Roth contributions, which means there may be two distinct types of money in the account: pre-tax deferrals (and any associated earnings) and Roth after-tax contributions (with tax-free growth). An order that treats both sources as a single undifferentiated pool creates implementation problems.

The profit sharing component adds another layer — employer contributions that may have separate vesting rules. The order should address whether the alternate payee's share is drawn from all sources or only specific ones, and whether vesting is relevant at the time of division.

QDRO Requirements

A qualifying domestic relations order for the FST Technical Services 401(k) Plan must satisfy ERISA's baseline requirements and the plan administrator's review procedures. At minimum, the order should:

  • Name the plan exactly: FST TECHNICAL SERVICES 401(K) PLAN.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee clearly enough for administrator review.
  • State the amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date or event controlling the calculation.
  • Address gains and losses from the valuation date through segregation or distribution.
  • Address Roth and pre-tax source treatment if both are present — typically a pro rata allocation.
  • Address participant loan treatment if a loan exists.
  • Avoid requiring any benefit form or option the plan does not provide.

Division Formulas

Percentage award

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for gains and losses through segregation. Specifying whether the percentage applies to all sources or only specified ones prevents ambiguity.

Fixed dollar amount

The alternate payee receives a specific dollar figure. The order should still address whether that amount adjusts for investment gains and losses between the valuation date and implementation.

Roth and pre-tax source allocation

Specify how each source is divided. A pro rata split — the same percentage of each source as the overall award — is the cleanest approach. Leaving source treatment silent creates implementation questions with real tax consequences.

Loan balance treatment

If there is an outstanding participant loan, state whether the award is calculated from the gross balance before the loan or the net balance after. That choice affects what the alternate payee actually receives.

Common Mistakes

  • Wrong plan name: using shorthand or informal language instead of the exact Form 5500 plan name.
  • Silent on Roth treatment: an order that ignores source allocation when both pre-tax and Roth money exist creates a problem the administrator has to solve — usually not in a way either party wanted.
  • No gains and losses language: participant-directed accounts move every day; the order should address investment changes after the valuation date.
  • Overlooking the loan: outstanding 401(k) loans directly affect how much the alternate payee actually receives.
  • Assuming ADP without confirming: third-party recordkeeper data can be stale; confirm with the sponsor before drafting to ADP procedures.

FAQ

Does dividing this plan in divorce require a QDRO?

Yes. A divorce decree alone cannot divide an ERISA 401(k) plan. A qualified domestic relations order is required before any alternate payee distribution.

Who processes QDROs for this plan?

Third-party sources point to ADP Retirement Services, but that was not confirmed from an official plan document. Verify the current recordkeeper directly with FST Technical Services before filing.

Why does the Roth balance matter so much?

Roth and pre-tax money have different tax treatment. An order that is silent on source allocation may cause the administrator to default to a method neither party intended, with irreversible tax consequences.

How do gains and losses work after the valuation date?

The account keeps moving with investment performance. The order should say whether the alternate payee's share adjusts for those changes between the valuation date and the date the account is actually segregated.

Where can I get current QDRO procedures for this plan?

Contact FST Technical Services directly. No official model QDRO or public plan-specific procedures were identified in the research used for this page.
