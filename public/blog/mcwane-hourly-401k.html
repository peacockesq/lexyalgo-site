About This Plan Type

The McWane Hourly 401(k) Retirement Plan is a defined contribution plan. That means eligible hourly employees contribute a portion of their wages — pre-tax, and possibly Roth after-tax — into individual participant accounts. McWane may make employer contributions such as a match or profit-sharing allocation on top of participant contributions. The account balance reflects actual contributions plus investment gains and losses over time.

The Form 5500 type codes for this plan indicate it includes 401(k) elective deferrals, profit-sharing features, and ESOP (Employee Stock Ownership Plan) elements. Plans with ESOP features may hold company stock, which can affect how the account is divided and what distribution options are available to an alternate payee. These details should be confirmed in the plan's Summary Plan Description.

Importantly, this plan is distinct from McWane's salaried plan and from any defined benefit (pension) plan McWane maintains. A QDRO must name the correct plan — using the wrong plan name in the order will result in rejection.

Dividing the McWane Hourly 401(k) in Divorce

Dividing a defined contribution plan in divorce involves identifying the marital portion of the account balance, agreeing on a division method, and preparing an order that satisfies ERISA and the plan's specific requirements.

The Marital Portion

The portion of the account that accrued during the marriage is generally considered marital property subject to division, though the exact definition depends on state law. Premarital contributions and the investment growth attributable to those premarital contributions may qualify as separate property in many jurisdictions. If the parties need to trace the premarital balance, they should request historical account data from McWane's benefits department or the plan recordkeeper.

Why a QDRO Is Required

The McWane Hourly 401(k) is a qualified plan under ERISA. Federal law prohibits plan administrators from paying retirement benefits to anyone other than the participant unless a valid QDRO has been accepted by the plan. A divorce decree alone — even one that awards a specific dollar amount — does not authorize the plan to make a distribution to the alternate payee.

Coverture fractions and 401(k) plans: A coverture fraction is a tool used to calculate what share of the account is marital property. It is an analysis method, not a distribution mechanism. The QDRO itself specifies the award (a percentage, a dollar amount, or a formula), and the plan administrator then calculates the actual value. Coverture fractions are most commonly used when there is a meaningful premarital balance to trace.

QDRO Requirements

A domestic relations order for the McWane Hourly 401(k) must satisfy ERISA's minimum requirements and any plan-specific procedures. At a minimum the order should:

  • Identify the plan by exact name: McWane Hourly 401(k) Retirement Plan.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient specificity for the plan administrator to process the order.
  • State the amount or percentage of the benefit being assigned, or the formula used to determine it.
  • Specify a clear valuation date or triggering event.
  • Address whether the alternate payee's share is adjusted for investment gains and losses after the valuation date.
  • Address any outstanding loan balance in the participant's account — loan treatment affects the net account value available for division.
  • If the plan includes an ESOP component with company stock, the order may need to address how company stock shares are treated.
  • Not require the plan to provide any benefit or option not otherwise available under the plan.

Division Formulas

Percentage of account balance

A percentage award is the most straightforward method: the alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for gains and losses until segregation. Example: "50% of the vested account balance as of [date], plus or minus proportionate earnings and losses thereafter."

Fixed dollar award

A specific dollar amount can work when the settlement already identifies a hard number. The order should still address gains and losses, or state that the dollar amount is frozen as of the valuation date. Leaving this unstated can create disputes if the market moves before the order is processed.

Marital share formula

Where there is a significant premarital balance to trace, the parties can build a formula into the QDRO: the alternate payee receives X% of the marital portion, with the marital portion defined by reference to the valuation date balance minus the premarital balance plus earnings attributable thereto. This approach requires solid historical account data.

Gains and losses

Whether or not gains and losses after the valuation date apply to the alternate payee's share should always be explicitly stated. Silence on this point creates implementation ambiguity in a moving-market account.

Common Mistakes

  • Wrong plan name: Using "McWane 401(k)" without specifying "Hourly" can result in rejection or processing delays.
  • Confusing the 401(k) with the pension: McWane has a separate defined benefit pension plan for hourly employees. The QDRO for the 401(k) is drafted very differently from a pension QDRO.
  • No gains and losses language: A 401(k) balance fluctuates with investment performance. Leaving gains and losses unaddressed creates ambiguity between the valuation date and the date of actual segregation.
  • Ignoring ESOP features: If the plan holds company stock through an ESOP component, the order may need to address how company stock shares are handled — this is not always covered by boilerplate 401(k) language.
  • Skipping administrator pre-review: Without a public model QDRO, a pre-submission review can prevent a costly rejection after court entry.

No. These are separate plans. The hourly 401(k), salaried 401(k), and the McWane Cast Iron Pipe Company Hourly Pension Plan are distinct. Confirm which plan the participant is enrolled in before drafting.

What part of the McWane 401(k) is marital property?

Generally, contributions and growth that accrued during the marriage are marital property under most state laws. Premarital balances and their attributable growth may be separate property. State law controls the exact analysis.
