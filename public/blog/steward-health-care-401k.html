The Bankruptcy and What It Means for the 401(k)

Steward Health Care's Chapter 11 bankruptcy raised serious concerns for many employees about retirement savings. The key distinction for 401(k) plan purposes:

  • Qualified 401(k) plan assets are legally separate from employer business assets under ERISA. These funds belong to the participants and are held in trust — they should not be available to pay employer creditors in a standard bankruptcy proceeding.
  • Non-qualified deferred compensation plans (funded through rabbi trusts) are different. Courts allowed approximately $60 million from non-qualified plans to be transferred to Steward to pay creditors. Participants in those non-qualified arrangements are treated as unsecured creditors and may recover only a fraction of what they are owed.

If the plan at issue in a divorce is the qualified 401(k) Retirement Savings Plan — not a non-qualified deferred compensation arrangement — the assets should be separately protected. However, employer matching contributions were suspended during financial distress, and plan administrative arrangements may have changed as hospital assets were sold to new operators. Always verify current status.

401(k) Plans in Divorce

The Steward Health Care 401(k) Retirement Savings Plan is a defined contribution plan. Each participant has an individual account that grows through employee contributions, employer contributions, and investment returns. In divorce, the account balance can be divided by dollar amount, percentage, or a formula tied to a specified valuation date.

The portion of the account that accrued during the marriage is generally subject to division under applicable state law. How that marital portion is calculated depends on the settlement, the court order, and state-specific rules. A time-based analysis (comparing months of marriage during plan participation to total participation) can identify the marital share — but the QDRO specifies the actual award. Those two things are distinct.

QDRO Requirements

A domestic relations order for the Steward Health Care 401(k) Retirement Savings Plan must meet ERISA's qualification requirements and the plan's current procedures. At minimum, the order should:

  • Use the correct plan name — Steward Health Care 401(K) Retirement Savings Plan — or whatever the current legal plan name is, confirmed by the current administrator.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient specificity for administrator review.
  • State the amount, percentage, or formula for the alternate payee's share.
  • Specify the valuation date.
  • Address investment gains and losses between the valuation date and account segregation.
  • Address any outstanding loan balance if applicable.
  • Avoid requiring any benefit not available under the plan.
Critical step for Steward plans: Because of the bankruptcy and hospital asset sales, confirm the plan is still active, identify who is currently administering it, and get current QDRO procedures before drafting. Do not rely on pre-2024 contact information without verification.

Division Formulas

Percentage formula

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the vested account balance as of the valuation date, adjusted for gains and losses until segregation. This is a common approach for 401(k) accounts with identifiable balances.

Fixed dollar formula

A specific dollar amount can be awarded, but the order should address whether it is adjusted for investment experience after the valuation date.

Gains and losses language

The QDRO should expressly say whether the alternate payee's share tracks investment returns from the valuation date through actual segregation. Silence on this point can create a dispute at implementation.

Marital share analysis

A time-based formula (months married during plan participation divided by total months of participation) is an analytical tool used to identify the marital portion of the account. It is not a distribution method — the QDRO itself specifies the award that results from that analysis.

Common Mistakes

  • Assuming pre-bankruptcy procedures still apply: Contact details, plan name, and QDRO procedures can all change following bankruptcy and asset sales. Verify everything before submitting.
  • Confusing the 401(k) with a non-qualified deferred compensation plan: Steward had both. Non-qualified plans were not ERISA-protected and were affected by the bankruptcy differently. Make sure you are targeting the correct plan.
  • Using an outdated plan name: Third-party sources reference an "Acquisition" variant of this plan name. Confirm the exact current legal name.
  • Skipping gains and losses language: Even in a straightforward 401(k) division, the account moves while the QDRO is in review. The order should address it.

Generally yes. ERISA-qualified 401(k) plan assets are held in trust and are legally separate from employer business assets. Non-qualified deferred compensation plans (rabbi trusts) are not protected. The distinction matters a great deal for Steward-related plans.
