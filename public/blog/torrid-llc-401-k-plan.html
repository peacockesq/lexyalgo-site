Torrid 401(k) in Divorce

The Torrid LLC 401(k) Plan is a defined contribution plan, meaning each participant holds an individual account rather than an entitlement to a future pension payment. In divorce, the account can typically be divided by percentage or fixed dollar amount as of a specific date. Because the account is invested, its value changes over time with market performance.

One background issue worth checking in any Torrid divorce case: Torrid was spun off from Hot Topic around 2019. If the divorce involves retirement benefits that were accumulated during or before that transition, confirm whether the account now sits in the Torrid LLC plan or a predecessor plan from the Hot Topic era. The current Form 5500 sponsor is Torrid LLC, EIN 47-3648005, and the plan has been operating continuously under that entity since at least 2020.

The plan type codes in Form 5500 include a profit sharing component, which means employer contributions may include a discretionary profit sharing element in addition to any matching. That detail can matter if the participant's account balance includes profit sharing funds that have distinct vesting rules.

QDRO Requirements

An order dividing the Torrid LLC 401(k) Plan should at minimum:

  • Identify the plan by its correct name: Torrid LLC 401(k) Plan.
  • Identify the participant and alternate payee with sufficient detail for John Hancock to locate and process the account.
  • Specify the exact amount, percentage, or formula used to determine the alternate payee's share.
  • State the valuation date used for the calculation.
  • Address whether the assigned share is adjusted for investment gains and losses between the valuation date and the date of actual segregation.
  • Avoid requiring any benefit type or form that the plan does not offer.

Division Formulas

Percentage formula

The alternate payee receives a stated percentage of the participant's vested account balance as of a valuation date. The most straightforward approach for a defined contribution plan like Torrid's 401(k). The order should state whether gains and losses apply from valuation through segregation.

Fixed dollar formula

A specific dollar amount is awarded. The order should still say whether that amount is adjusted for investment performance between the valuation date and the date John Hancock transfers the funds. A frozen amount and a gains-and-losses-adjusted amount can produce very different outcomes.

Vesting treatment

Profit sharing contributions may have a vesting schedule separate from elective deferrals. If the participant is not yet fully vested in some employer contributions, the order should clarify whether the alternate payee's share is drawn from vested balances only or from the full account including unvested amounts, subject to whatever vesting later occurs.

Gains and losses

Between the valuation date and actual segregation, the account balance moves with market performance. An explicit statement in the order — whether the alternate payee share tracks gains and losses or is frozen at the valuation date amount — prevents avoidable implementation disputes.

Steps to Divide

  1. Confirm the plan is the Torrid LLC 401(k) Plan — verify the current account statement or benefits records reflect Torrid LLC as the employer and John Hancock as recordkeeper.
  2. Check whether any amounts in the account predate the Torrid/Hot Topic spin-off, and confirm those amounts are now held under this plan.
  3. Gather the latest account statement showing all sources: pre-tax deferrals, Roth, employer match, and any profit sharing.
  4. Decide on the division method: percentage or fixed dollar, with gains and losses language.
  5. Draft the QDRO and submit for John Hancock review before court entry, if possible.
  6. After court entry, submit the certified order and follow through until John Hancock confirms the alternate payee account is established.

Common Mistakes

  • Wrong plan name: Using "Torrid 401(k)" without the "LLC" may create ambiguity — use the exact plan name from current benefits records.
  • Ignoring the spin-off history: If the divorce period straddles 2019–2021, confirm whether account balances trace to Torrid LLC or a Hot Topic predecessor plan.
  • Omitting gains and losses: Silence on investment performance between valuation and segregation creates avoidable disputes at implementation.
  • Using a generic 401(k) order: John Hancock has plan-specific review procedures; a boilerplate order from a different plan may be rejected or require amendment.
  • Skipping pre-review: Without a public model QDRO, a draft submission to John Hancock before court entry saves time and avoids re-filings.

FAQ

Does the Torrid LLC 401(k) Plan require a QDRO for divorce division?

Yes. An ERISA-governed 401(k) plan requires a qualified domestic relations order before the plan administrator will transfer benefits to an alternate payee. A divorce judgment alone is not sufficient.

Who recordkeeps the Torrid LLC 401(k) Plan?

John Hancock is identified in secondary sources as the recordkeeper. John Hancock handles QDRO review and alternate payee account establishment for the plans it administers. Contact them at 1-800-294-3575 or 1-833-388-6466 to request current procedures for this specific plan.

Does the Torrid spin-off from Hot Topic matter?

It can. Torrid was spun off around 2019. If a divorce involves retirement savings from before that period, confirm that all account balances are now held under the current Torrid LLC plan and not a legacy Hot Topic plan. The plan sponsor is currently Torrid LLC, EIN 47-3648005.

How should gains and losses be handled in the QDRO?

Explicitly. The order should state whether the alternate payee's assigned amount is adjusted for investment gains and losses from the valuation date through the date of actual segregation. Silence on this point typically leads to implementation disputes.

Is there a public model QDRO for the Torrid 401(k)?

No official public model QDRO was found in the research used for this page. Contact John Hancock or Torrid HR to confirm current QDRO procedures before drafting and submitting an order.
